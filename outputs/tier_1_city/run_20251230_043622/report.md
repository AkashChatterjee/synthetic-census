## Synthesized Article

### **Americans Hesitant to Judge India's Economic Policy—But Divided on Safety Nets at Home**

When asked about universal minimum income for India, Americans across professional backgrounds share an unexpected trait: humility. From high school teachers to radiologists to truck drivers, the consistent refrain is "that's outside my expertise"—a striking departure from the confident hot-takes that dominate social media discourse.

Yet beneath this epistemic caution lies a revealing fault line about economic security closer to home. The question of guaranteed income, even framed as a distant policy debate, exposes deep divisions in how Americans think about work, poverty, and government's role in economic life.

Education and healthcare professionals tend toward cautious support for safety net concepts, though they're careful to distinguish between theory and implementation. A Chicago-area teacher articulates what she sees daily: students unable to focus on learning because they're worried about family finances or working 20-hour weeks to help with rent. "Poverty is the biggest predictor of educational outcomes I see," she notes, suggesting that any policy addressing this deserves consideration. A Boston radiologist similarly observes how economic insecurity leads patients to delay care until conditions become critical—medically and economically inefficient.

These professionals don't claim expertise in development economics, but their frontline experience with poverty's consequences shapes their intuition that baseline economic security might address root causes they witness daily.

Working-class perspectives reveal more skepticism, though not uniformly. A Tennessee truck driver frames guaranteed income through the lens of taxation and work ethic: "I've worked my ass off for 22 years... Nobody handed me nothing." His concern isn't just fiscal—it's moral. "Work gives you purpose," he argues, worrying that guaranteed income would "make people lazy and kill their pride."

A Phoenix retail manager occupies conflicted middle ground. She sees customers "choosing between groceries and medicine" and recognizes that baseline security "would be life-changing." Yet she also wonders whether guaranteed income would simply let companies pay even less, or whether "people who could work but don't" would be enabled rather than helped. Her ambivalence reflects genuine working-class tension: experiencing economic precarity while valuing work's dignity.

Interestingly, the AI displacement narrative—often central to UBI advocacy—barely registers. Despite autonomous vehicles threatening trucking jobs and AI potentially automating radiology, these workers don't frame safety nets through self-preservation. The teacher is the only persona consistently connecting automation to economic policy, suggesting the AI threat remains more abstract than immediate for most workers.

Technical professionals approach the question through implementation rather than ideology. A software engineer focuses on systems challenges—identity verification at scale, distribution infrastructure, fraud prevention—while questioning whether guaranteed income addresses root needs better than investments in infrastructure and education.

What emerges is less a clear pro-or-con consensus than a values-based divide: those who see poverty as a systemic barrier requiring structural solutions versus those who view economic security as something earned through work, with government assistance risking dependency. The India framing may have muted these tensions, but they simmer just beneath the surface—ready to ignite when the conversation turns homeward.
